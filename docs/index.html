01 — Motivation & Research Question

Why do contracts change the way firms bid?

Electricity markets are prone to market power: generators can profitably withhold capacity to push spot prices above competitive levels. Forward contracts — either PPAs (bilateral, price-fixing) or CfDs (government-backed, reference-price) — alter this incentive by reducing a firm's residual exposure to spot prices. The degree of hedging, f, is therefore central to pricing outcomes.

"A generator with forward commitments bids differently — it has already locked in revenue, so the marginal incentive to inflate spot prices weakens."

Research Question

How does the level and type of forward contracting (PPAs vs CfDs) affect equilibrium bidding functions and resulting spot prices? And does UK market data support the theoretical predictions?

02 — Theoretical Finding I

Finding 1

Bidding is decreasing in f — but only up to a threshold f*

As contract coverage rises, generators shade their bids downward — they bear less residual spot-market exposure. However, this effect is non-monotone: beyond a threshold f* (which varies with renewable capacity r), the bid function flattens or reverses. Each colour corresponds to one level of r; the vertical dashed line marks its f*.

b(f, r) = MC + [P(Q–r) – MC] · (1–f) / (1 + 1/N)


f*(r) = 1 – (K–r)/Q · (1 + 1/N)


N = number of firms, K = thermal capacity, r = renewable output, Q = demand. Parameters calibrated to UK market.

03 — Theoretical Finding II

Finding 2

PPA leaves market outcomes unchanged; CfD retains a price effect

Under a PPA, the contract price nets out of the generator's payoff — spot market bidding is unaffected regardless of coverage. Under a CfD, the strike price creates an asymmetric incentive: higher coverage still compresses bids. The figure plots bid against r; the dashed line is PPA (invariant benchmark), solid lines are CfD at different coverage levels.
